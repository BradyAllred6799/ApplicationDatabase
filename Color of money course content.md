## Part 1: Course Introduction

### Course Intro and Objectives

The Color of Money: A Guide to University Funds

Organizations with a clear mission are more successful because it unifies their efforts. The University of Florida's mission is to empower learners, advance knowledge, and strengthen communities through teaching, research, and service.

Our mission gains support from a variety of funding sources to whom we must be accountable as a public institution. We must always ensure purchases are reasonable, allowable, and follow established guidelines.

At UF, accountability involves stewardship and fiscal responsibility. Every dollar spent should support our mission while maintaining transparency. This course will teach you to:

- Explain the purpose of fund accounting at UF

- Identify the four major types of funds

- Recognize exceptions to the spending rules

- Distinguish between budget- and cash-based funds

- Identify current and carry-forward funds

Mastering these principles empowers you to uphold the mission and accountability standards of the University of Florida.

### Where Does Our Money Come From?

UF receives funding from these four major sources. We will refer to them as the colors of money.

- State Appropriations

- Sponsored Projects

- Component Units

- Fee-for-Service Funds

### Fund Accounting

Fund accounting is the method UF uses to segregate its financial resources into distinct "colored buckets". This allows the University to track each source separately and ensure compliance with restrictions of the various sources. Mandated by the Government Accounting Standards Board, fund accounting ensures resources are spent for their intended purposes and are accounted for/reported on accurately. Departments spend from/make deposits into these "buckets".

- State Appropriations: Budget-Based, Fund Codes: 101, 102, 103

- Sponsored Projects: Budget-Based, Fund Codes: 201, 209, 214

- Component Units: Cash-Based, Fund Codes: 171, 213

- Fee-for-Service: Cash-Based, Fund Codes: 14x, 15x, 16x

### State Appropriations

UF is part of the State University System (SUS), which serves over 300,000 students and is the second largest system in the country. Funding for the SUS comes from the state's general revenue, lottery funds, student tuition and other trust funds. These funds whether allocated from the state or collected locally (like tuition) are governed by state regulations.

The Board of Governors has established a Performance-Based Funding Model for SUS schools. The University is evaluated on a range of issues including budget alignment, exceptional faculty and academic programs, and improving students’ success during and post graduation.

### Sponsored Projects

We refer to contracts, grants and cooperative agreements as Sponsored Projects. We receive federal and non-federal money (awards) from granting agencies, typically to support research activities. Faculty (and others) put together proposals which result in monetary awards that enable researchers to execute and bring groundbreaking research to life.

In FY25 UF faculty expended over $1.33 billion in research expenditures and had over $612 million in Federal funding.

### Component Units

These are affiliated organizations that exist as separate legal entities but have significant relationships with the University and are included in the University's financial statements. They include Direct Support Organizations whose primary purpose is to support the University (e.g. UF Foundation, University Athletic Association), and other affiliates (Faculty Practice Plans, Hospitals).

The University Enterprise (One UF) is comprised of 27 component units. The component units receive funding from a variety of sources including donations, sales and services, hospital revenues, royalties, and others. UF alone has almost $7B in assets. If we include all the component units, it would bring the total to almost $16B.

### Fee-for-Service Funds

Revenue-generating activities from sales of goods or services. Customers can be internal (paying with UF fund source) or external to UF (paying with non-UF fund source). Examples of customers are departments, students, employees, other universities or not-for-profits, other for-profit entities, and members of the community.

Fee-for-Service Activities have been categorized into four types: Auxiliaries (Housing, Parking and Transportation), Research Service Centers (Animal Care, ICBR), Cultural and Broadcasting (Harn Museum, WUFT), and External Business Activities (testing labs for primarily external agencies).

Designated Auxiliaries are required by FL statute to be self-supporting, however, many of the other Fee-for-Service Activities will require funding beyond the revenues they generate to sustain operations. Revenues generated by Fee-for-Service Activities are to be used for expenses related to the business purpose of the activity.

### Spending at UF: Broad Principles

UF has consistent spending rules regardless of funding source. Before we dive into the specifics of the Colors of Money, we will first review the core principles that govern most university expenditures.

### 5Ws of the IRS – Business Purpose Documentation

Documentation must show the IRS “five W’s” for business expenses; without those details payments are taxable and must be reported on the employee’s W‑2.

**Who:** The name of the individual who incurred the expense: Example: John Smith

**What:** What the expense entailed. Example: flight tickets to New York

**Where:** The location, including city, state, and country. Example: New York, NY, USA

**When:** The date the travel, meal, or entertainment occurred. Example: June 18, 2025

**Why:** Topic discussed, business activity, or agenda, and how it relates to UF/fund charged. Example: collaborate with Superhero Peter Parker from Marvel Comics on impact of spider webs on street congestion for DOT study on impact of traffic obstructions.

**Full Example:** “John Smith’s flight to New York, NY, USA on June 18, 2025 to collaborate with Superhero Peter Parker from Marvel Comics on impact of spider webs on street congestion for DOT study on impact of traffic obstructions.” vs. “Trip to meet with P. Parker.”

### Employee Item Rules For ANY Color of Money

Regardless of the funding source, money cannot be expended to satisfy the personal preference of employees. Ask yourself, "Is it necessary to carry out UF business purposes or is this item based on a personal employee preference?"

Exceptions: Items that typically fall in this category can be purchased if justification is obtained when the items are for the organization’s convenience. For example, a refrigerator can be purchased for a break room, but not for a personal office (even if the refrigerator is small).

Justifications: A letter of justification for these allowed purchases will be required. Using State Fund Codes 101-103 is discouraged. Items cannot be for personal use or in the personal space of an employee. If the item is for personal use for the organization’s convenience, however, it may be approved as a perquisite.

Perquisites: What’s a perquisite? (Think “job perk”). Goods or services that are required by the employer, which may be given or provided at reduced cost to employees based on position requirements. Typical perquisites include uniforms, special footwear, and safety equipment.

### Business Rules For ANY Color of Money

Regardless of funding source, all transactions must comply with:

- Competitive procurement processes (including bid/quote limits; for more details, please visit [Procurement Directives and Procedures](https://procurement.ufl.edu/directives-procedures/))

- Contract and requisition business rules

- Limitations on unencumbered expenses

- Travel directives on reimbursements, including pre-approval and reimbursement in myUFL

### Political and Charitable Contributions

- Payments to Political Action Committees (PACs) when included within a membership fee are allowed.

**·** **No other political contributions allowed.**

- Lobbying fees are also allowed for Fund Code 171.

- Charitable contributions are allowed for Fund Code 171, Source of Funds, and Faculty Practice Plans.

### Never Allowed

Regardless of Funding Source, money may NOT be used for:

- Private club memberships

- Lavish or extravagant entertainment

- Personal benefit to an individual (other than an incidental benefit)

### General Rules For ANY Color of Money

Rules for how ANY Color of Money may be spent (regardless of funding source):

- It must have a clear business purpose

- It must be for relevant and appropriate operating expenses

- All disbursements and expenditures must also have written documentation suitable for auditing purposes (accountability)

## Part 2: State Appropriations

### Overview of State Appropriations

State appropriations, also called State Funds, Education and General (E&G), or General Revenue, are [budget‑based](#_Budget-Based_Funds) funds appropriated to UF by the State Legislature. These funds must be used for operating expenses and must follow State of Florida restrictions; typical allowable operating expenses include salaries, office supplies, and travel.

### Fund Codes You'll See

Most common State Appropriation fund codes: 101 (main campus), 102 (HSC), 103 (IFAS). Other state‑related funds include 107 (state preeminence) and 106 (UF Online).

Fund Codes 111, 112, 113, and 122 capture lottery funds and student fees as tuition is collected. When recording current‑year spending, use the Budget Reference “CRRNT” and be mindful of [current budget vs. carryforward](#_Current_Budget_vs.).

### Allowable, Reasonable, and the “Why”

Funds must be spent for relevant operating expenses and must follow State of Florida restrictions.

- “Allowable” means the expense is allowable or not specifically excluded by government regulations.

- “Reasonable” means a prudent person would have purchased the item and paid that price.

- The “why” behind restrictions: as a taxpayer, would you want to pay for alcoholic beverages for other people?

Always adhere to state and university guidelines and consult the budget office for clarification. Allowable expenses must be justified and reasonable. Remember to use your managerial judgment and remain vigilant about fund usage to ensure compliance.

### Funding Exceptions

- Alcoholic Beverages: Not allowed under any circumstances.

- Food Items: Only allowed for recruitment purposes.

- Gifts or Gift Cards: Allowable for retirement or merit awards up to $100.

- Party Items: Not allowed under any circumstances.

- Fines and Penalties: Not allowed under any circumstances.

- Member Dues: Allowed only if separate from Political Action Committees (PACs). Generally, should be for organizational memberships determined essential to the duties of the organization.

- Political Action Committees: Not allowed under any circumstances.

### Other Non-Allowable Purchases

Some other common examples of items that are not normally allowed to be purchased or paid for with State Appropriations include:

- First-class travel

- Greeting cards and flowers

- Entertainment for university guests

- Various decorations

- Picture frames or wall hangings

- Portable heaters or fans

- Coffee pots and supplies

- Stoves and microwaves

- Refrigerators

- Plaques for outstanding service

## Part 3: Sponsored Projects

### Overview of Sponsored Projects at UF

UF receives significant outside funding for research via sponsored projects, contracts, grants, and cooperative agreements. Projects are proposed by a PI to an external agency; when funded, they become contractual agreements approved by UF and governed by sponsored‑project regulations. Budgets are “budget-based” and follow the grant period (not the university fiscal year). UF lingo you’ll see: PI (Principal Investigator), PM (Project Manager), C&G (Contracts & Grants) and DSP (Division of Sponsored Programs).

### Types of Awards

- Contract: A legally binding agreement where the recipient agrees to perform defined tasks or deliverables in exchange for payment.

- Grant: A financial assistance award given to support a public purpose such as research, education, or community development. The sponsor provides funding based on a proposal submitted.

- Cooperative Agreement: Similar to a grant but involves substantial involvement from the sponsor during the performance of the funded activity.

### Roles, UFIRST, and Accountability

Establishing an award in UF systems is a shared responsibility of the PI, their department, and the Core office. UFIRST captures the award and its terms/conditions (e.g., deliverables, extra stipulations). Sponsored project funds must be used to directly support the scope agreed upon in the award. Every charge needs a clear business purpose, and sponsors expect reporting on how the dollars were spent.

### Direct vs. Indirect (F&A) Costs

Direct Costs: Direct costs are specifically associated with and identifiable to a particular project (e.g., PI labor on the project, lab supplies, direct materials). Direct dollars are spent by the PI to support the scope of work. Some costs are unallowable and must be paid by another funding source.

Indirect (F&A) Costs: Indirect (F&A) costs are to offset the cost of managing UF's research enterprise. F&A costs benefit multiple activities and aren't assignable without disproportionate effort (e.g., clerical salaries, utilities, general office expenses). F&A dollars go to UF Research and are then distributed to applicable units.

The Uniform Guidance incorporates the [Cost Accounting Standards (CAS),](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/48/part-9905) which establishes the principles that costs will be consistently treated as a direct or indirect (F&A) costs.

### Spending Sponsored Funds

Specific funds within sponsored projects also carry their own unique rules and regulations. This includes:

- Federal (201) and Nonfederal (209) Direct Costs

- Return Overhead (211) and Residual Funds (212)

- IFAS Federal Funds (223)

### Fund Codes 201/209

Fund Codes for direct costs: 201 (federal) and 209 (non-federal: state, private, other). All activities and expenses must specifically benefit the award, and a clearly stated business purpose is essential. Apply State Appropriation guidelines unless the sponsor specifically allows or disallows otherwise, follow the stricter rule. When spending sponsored project funds, the seventh ChartField “Source of Fund” is required.

What Are Typically Direct Charges on Federal (201)? Generally direct charges on 201, if relevant to the project: salaries for research-related positions, lab supplies, travel, technical/scientific equipment, animal care and other cost center services, repair/maintenance for equipment purchased, long-distance calls related to the project. Generally not charged directly on 201: clerical/secretarial/administrative salaries, data processing/computer supplies, basic postage and telephone, office supplies, general software, subscriptions, institutional/individual memberships.

What About Non-Federal (209) Charging? On 209, all costs may be directly charged if they have sponsor approval and are relevant to the project. Like State funds, do not charge food, alcohol, party items, or gifts. Direct questions to your college’s Contracts & Grants office.

### Returned Overhead (211) and Residual Funds (212): Research-Only Dollars

Both 211 and 212 are cash-based and must be used for research-related activities; restrictions are relaxed compared to original awards, and these funds “turn colors” from purple to pink.

Returned Overhead (211): Each year, some indirects are returned to colleges, departments, and PIs. By statute (F.S. 1004.22), overhead first pays “off-the-top” costs to operate the division of sponsored research; any sponsored research and development surplus supports other research or sponsored training programs. 211 follows state guidelines; you may buy food for a research‑related meeting; no alcohol or party items.

Residual Funds (212): Unused funds not needed to complete a sponsored project; some residuals must go back to the sponsor. Funds not returned go to the PI, their work unit, or the appropriate vice president. Closeout forms can set up new residual projects. 212 is treated the same as 211 (research‑only; can buy food if research-related; no alcohol/party items).

Examples of Research-Related Activities:

- Appropriate: Graduate research assistant salaries, research lab equipment, travel to present a paper.

- Inappropriate: Classroom supplies, refreshments for a staff holiday party

### IFAS Federal Funds (223)

As a land-grant institution, UF receives federal funds for IFAS in addition to State Appropriations and Sponsored Projects. These funds (Fund 223, IFAS Capacity) are allocated at the discretion of the appropriate IFAS dean and are governed by federal guidelines.

### Funding Exceptions

- Alcoholic Beverages: Only if part of the research project.

- Food Items: Only if included in the scope of the grant or Fund 211 if related to the research project.

- Gifts or Gift Cards: Only if gifts are used to support the research.

- Party Items: Not allowed under any circumstances.

- Fines and Penalties: Not allowed under any circumstances.

- Member Dues: Generally NOT Allowed. Others - Allowable if Separate from PAC.

## Part 5: Fee-for-Service Units

### Overview of Fee-for-Service (FFS) Funds

FFS funds come from selling goods and services to customers at UF—employees, departments, students, and the community. They are unrestricted funds generated by your unit and designated by the department head in support of UF’s mission. FFS operations run similar to a private business: you have discretion to define programmatic needs (with some limits), and every expense must be documented for audit.

“Unrestricted” Doesn’t Mean “Anything Goes”. Spend only on operating needs that directly advance your unit’s program (for example, salaries, office supplies, travel).

### Governance And The Core Rule

FFS funds are generally governed by state appropriations guidelines, with some exceptions. They may be expended for any goods and services necessary to fulfill the mission and program needs of the FFS unit or auxiliary. ## Appendix

### A. Glossary: Fiscal Terminology and Key Concepts

#### Fund Accounting

Fund accounting is the method UF uses to segregate its financial resources into distinct "colored buckets". This allows the University to track each source separately and ensure compliance with restrictions of the various sources. Mandated by the Government Accounting Standards Board, fund accounting ensures resources are spent for their intended purposes and are accounted/reported on accurately. Departments spend from/make deposits into these "buckets".

- State Appropriations: Budget-Based, Fund Codes: 101, 102, 103

- Sponsored Projects: Budget-Based, Fund Codes: 201, 209, 214

- Component Units: Cash-Based, Fund Codes: 171, 213

- Fee-for-Service: Cash-Based, Fund Codes: 14x, 15x, 16x

#### Cash-Based Funds

Cash-based funding equals beginning cash plus revenue minus expenditures. You can spend only after the cash has been received—think of it like your wallet: you must have the cash before you spend it. This is not a fixed appropriation—spending is limited by actual cash on hand. (Component Units, Fee-for-Service)

#### Budget-Based Funds

UF provides lump-sum allocations to the appropriate Vice Presidents and Deans, who may further distribute them to departments and divisions. Once your allocation is made, those budget‑based funds can be spent. Think of budget-based funding as a credit card limit extended and guaranteed to your department: you can spend what is in your “bucket,” but you cannot go over; the bucket is finite unless central leadership reallocates funds. The UF CFO and the Provost’s Office, working with VPs and UF Deans, decide how much of this “bucket” can be used by your department. A larger department may have a larger bucket; a smaller department may have a smaller bucket. (State Appropriations, Sponsored Projects)

#### Fund Code

A Fund Code is the three-digit ChartField you must enter on every myUFL transaction to designate which self-balancing fund—state appropriations, fee-for-service revenues, sponsored projects, etc.—is being used. By tagging each transaction with its Fund Code, UF enforces the specific spending controls, restrictions and reporting requirements tied to that funding source, ensuring accountability to government bodies, donors and other stakeholders. For example: Fund code 101 is for Main Campus.

#### Source of Funds

A “Source of Funds” is a required seven-digit ChartField in myUFL/PeopleSoft that identifies the external agency or entity providing revenue and tracks related expenditures. By tagging contracts, grants and DSO/affiliate transfers with this Code, UF ensures each dollar is reported and spent according to donor or agency requirements. For example G000020 = US Department of Education grant.

#### myUFL

University of Florida’s name for the PeopleSoft system (and related tools) that we use for financial, human resources, student financials, and reporting transactions.

#### ChartField

A ChartField is a standardized numeric code that serves as the backbone of UF’s accounting system, structuring every financial transaction. When combined correctly—typically guided by the fund code—it ensures accurate revenue and expense reporting, which in turn drives strategic planning, shapes budget policy decisions, and upholds the University’s credibility.

##### ChartField Components

- Budget Ref: 5 characters, Identifies the components of the State appropriations budget for the Fiscal Year, Required for all transactions but will only vary from CRRNT in Appropriations Funds, Same restrictions apply to Current and Carryforward funds - the color of money does not change!

- Program Code: 4 digits, Identifies the functional nature of the expense - how expenses support UF through either: instruction, research, administrative, or academic support, The first two digits represent the function of the expense.

- Account Code: 6 digits, Classifies financial activity within the general ledger, Tells you what the transaction was for, You will recognize these Account Codes in Monthly Financial Reports.

- Dept ID: 8 digits, Identifies the budgetary unit or responsibility center for the financial transaction, Represents ownership of those funds and transactions.

- Fund Code: 3 digits, Tracks University financial transactions and supports accountability, Required field for all transactions, Categorizes source, intended purpose of money and how they should be spent.

- Business Unit: 5 Characters, Represents legal entity for reporting purposes, Most units typically use UFLOR.

#### Funds are Self-Balancing

At UF, all funds are self-balancing—that is, each fund code has its own set of ledgers into which every dollar of revenue and expense is posted so the accounts always reconcile to a closing balance. This setup isolates each fund’s cash flows, enforces any donor or state restrictions and spending rules, and makes tracking and auditing each fund straightforward.

#### Current Budget vs. Carryforward

Current budget: Spend the appropriation in the year the funds were budgeted (note the fiscal year). When spending current budget, place “CRRNT” in the Budget Reference field. The Budget Reference ChartField answers, “When did I receive this money?”

Carryforward: Unexpended balances on June 30 are carried forward and budgeted in the next fiscal year; same restrictions as State Appropriations, and may be used for recurring expenses (e.g., regular salaries). Be cautious when using carryforward funds for recurring expenses, as they are limited. Plan spending to avoid unspent balances. Use Carryforward funds for summer salaries, OPS (short-term projects), or non-recurring expenses like software implementation.
