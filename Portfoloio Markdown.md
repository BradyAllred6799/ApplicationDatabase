## Automating WCAG Compliance: Converting 800+ Guides With AI

Skill Categories: *AI Automation, Python Scripting & Process Scalability*

I integrated AI and Python scripting to convert over 800 inaccessible PDF instruction guides into WCAG‑compliant DOCX files, saving my design team months of manual work.

 ![A python coiled around a large, blue letter "W" with a blurred background of greenery and sunlight.](image.png)

### Automating WCAG Compliance: The Problem

Florida regulations require all university online resources to meet WCAG 2.2 standards by April 2026.

#### Automating WCAG Compliance: Design Story

A needs analysis led us to move from PDF to DOCX with a custom style guide. Without automation, eight designers would have spent countless hours reworking existing guides.

#### Automating WCAG Compliance: Complexity

The team’s time was limited and in high demand. A manual transition was not feasible at the required scale and timeline.

*Have you ever wondered exactly how many students view the open-access pages that we design? In the next few minutes, I'll show you a simple and powerful way that you can track exactly which pages our students visit. Stick around, and I'll walk you through every step of using Wix to gather real-time analytics in Canvas.

Let's kick this off by emphasizing why we need a workaround in the first place. Normally, in a closed canvas course, we can track which pages our students visit, how often, and how long they're there. However, once you open that canvas course for anyone to see, whether they're officially enrolled or just browsing, the standard Canvas analytics tools can't capture that data. This means we're left guessing how much our content actually gets used. So here's the big idea. We're going to embed an invisible iframe on each of our canvas pages that leads to a blank Wix page. Because Wix provides automatic analytics for each of its websites, every time a student opens a canvas page containing that hidden iframe, the visit is recorded in Wix. It's a seamless solution that runs behind the scenes. No one sees it, but the data flows to your Wix dashboard, giving you a snapshot of who is visiting, when, and how often. Now you might be asking, won't this impact the students' experience? Actually, they'll never notice. Your course design will remain the same, but you'll gain the ability to see how students are interacting with your Canvas course. This gives you a massive advantage when iterating on your design. Let's take a look and see how it's all set up.

First things first, head to wix.com(opens in a new tab) and log in. Choose "continue with Google," and select the BYU online TA design team account. If you're not signed in or can't see the design team Google account, find the login credentials in the team onboarding course. Now pause for a second. Instead of starting from scratch, find an existing Wix site on our dashboard that you can duplicate. Once you're logged in, you'll see a list of our team's current sites. Look for an already published site that you can duplicate. Usually, this can be the example site. Look for the site actions button and select duplicate site. Name the site after the canvas course you're trying to track. In this case, we could name it Sandbox Tracking. Select duplicate and let Wix work its magic. You might ask, Why is this so important? By skipping over Wix's website creation process, you get a blank website to work with instantaneously and cut our setup time in half.

Now that you have your duplicated site, it's time to get it ready for canvas tracking. In the Wix dashboard, find the newly duplicated site and select edit site. This takes you into a new dashboard specific to this website. We'll return here later to look at the site's analytics, but for now, select the design site button on the top right corner. This opens up the Wix drag-and-drop editor. If you're not familiar with the editor, don't worry, you only need to do a few simple actions here. You'll notice this site may come with a home page or maybe some other tracking pages that were duplicated. Usually, we keep the home page, then remove any other pages that aren't relevant to our new course. You can do this by opening the pages and menu tab on the left side. And deleting anything that isn't relevant to the course. Be careful, though. Always leave the homepage, as this will become the tracker for the home page of your Canvas course.

Now, for every page in your Canvas course that you want to track, create a corresponding blank page in Wix. While in the pages and menu tab, click Add Page at the bottom left-hand corner of your menu list. Select Add blank page. And name the page as closely as possible to the canvas page that you're wanting to track. This will help you later when you're embedding iframes. Repeat this process for as many pages as you're intending to track in your Canvas course. By the end of this step, your Wix pages and menu should match your Canvas course's structure. Remember, you don't need to create a Wix page for every single Canvas page in your course, only the Canvas pages that you want to track.

Once your pages are set up and your menu is in place, click Save. Wix will prompt you with a default URL. Be sure to rename that URL to reflect your Canvas course. For us, that'll be sandbox tracking. Make sure the name change sticks by hitting the check mark on the right. And hitting publish now. Why publish now? Because once the site is live, the Wix analytics start to function, and we can copy the actual URLs that we'll be embedding in Canvas.

Here's the most critical part: making the iframe invisible yet powerful for data collection. Now view your published site in a new tab. You'll see the home page is blank except for a menu. Go to each page in the menu to see that there is a unique URL associated with each menu item. You'll need each URL when creating specific iframes for tracking your different Canvas pages. IFrames are basically little windows that show one web page inside another. In our case, it's a blank Wix page inside our Canvas course. But look carefully at this snippet of code. Style equals display none, hides the iframe visually, and aria hidden equals true tells screen readers to ignore this iframe. This ensures the iframe is completely invisible and doesn't disrupt accessibility or user experience.

Now we need to start placing our trackers in specific canvas pages. Open the first page you want to track. And click on it. Open the advanced HTML editor. And paste the iframe code at the very top of the page above the wrapper. This code can be found in the description of the video. Now we need to replace the placeholder text with the actual URL from the page that will be tracking this canvas page. Navigate to your published Wix website. And copy the corresponding URL. Return to the canvas editor and paste it in place. Once it's in place, we can hit save. And Wix will begin to track analytics from this Canvas page. Repeat this process for each page in your Canvas course. As a result, when a Canvas page loads, the Wix iframe will load as well, causing Wix to send a page view to your analytics. But you have to make sure that the URL in the iframe matches the URL in the Wix web page tracking the Canvas page. If you get those URLs mixed up, your data will be incorrect.

Once all of your iframes are embedded, you're ready to see the magic in action. Navigate to your website's dashboard and find the analytics option in the left-hand menu. Select real time. This real-time analytics view is incredibly handy for testing your eye frames. Open your canvas pages in a separate window or ask a colleague to open them. Stop for a minute. You will need to wait for the pages to load. It can take up to 2 minutes for a new page view to register. Be patient and watch the data appear. Each page in your Wx site will show how many visitors are currently viewing it, including location data, if available. Over time, you'll see how many people viewed each Canvas page through the Wix Analytics. This gives you a great idea of which pages in your Canvas course are being used and where you can focus your improvements.

That's it. You've learned how to set up a robust behind-the-scenes analytics system so you can track how your students are using your open-access course. So what's next? I identified the need for a test plan that ensures proper coverage of eight major content areas (e.g., biopsychology, social psychology) while also measuring knowledge, understanding, application, analysis, and evaluation.

#### Assessment Plan Blueprint: Complexity

Balancing multiple cognitive levels demanded a structured blueprint, varied item types, and tight alignment with clear SLOs.

In this project, I also integrated AI tools (ChatGPT) to generate initial drafts of test items. These AI-generated ideas provided a diverse starting point, which I refined and aligned with rigorous item-writing guidelines.
